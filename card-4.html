Vignette

A parent channel regularly posts the child as the “face” of content, including sponsored items or crowdfunding appeals. The child's image, routines, and reactions drive engagement and income.

Forces & Risks

  • Income & reach vs. autonomy & future dignity
  • Parasocial closeness vs. traceability & copying
  • Platform growth nudges vs. assent boundaries

Risk path: exposure → increased accessibility/remix → reputational/emotional harm later
Contextual Integrity: family context leaking into branded/public performance space

Prompts

Rights: Your child is the featured talent—ask first; limit audience if unsure.

Risk: Sponsored posts spread further. Reduce copying, or keep private?

Norms: Many creators keep kids' faces private and use non-identifying thumbnails.

Assent

Helpers

  • Sponsored content ; assent still required.
  • Non-identifying thumbnail ; avoid faces/names/school logos.
  • Trim discomfort (remove tears, meltdowns).
  • Save to draft and ask when the child is present.
  • Check-in later (24–48h) before publishing a sponsor post.
  • Remix Off • Downloads Off • Location Off
  • Close Friends ; consider alt formats (hands only, voice-over).
  • Non-identifying thumbnail; trim discomfort segments.
  • Remix/Downloads/Location Off
  • Private/Close Friends ; keep as draft unless assent changes.
  • Comments Off • Remix Off • Downloads Off • Location Off
  • Check-in later before any future brand post.
Bystanders & co-ownership: blur peers/family and brand staff if consent is unknown.

Law & Policy

“Children have a right to be heard.” — UNCRC GC No. 12 “High privacy by default.” — ICO Children's Code Assent in pediatrics — AAP (2016) / AAP (1995)

Brand deals or fundraising do not negate assent. Keep controls strict when uncertainty or risk is high.

Evidence hooks

  • Stephenson et al., 2024: Creator norms + algorithmic reach = wider reuse; child-featured content travels fastest.
  • Borgos-Rodriguez et al., 2019: Disability family channels trade authenticity for visibility; identity risks accumulate over time.
  • Sousa, 2011: “Warrior-hero” narratives can overshadow the child's agency; future self-presentation concerns.
  • Steinberg, 2016: Children's interests deserve independent weight—sponsorship never overrides assent.
