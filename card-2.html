Vignette

To cover treatment/travel costs, a parent posts diagnosis details, lab results, and a public crowdfunding link. The post spreads quickly; the child’s medical story becomes searchable and reusable beyond the family’s control.

Forces & Risks

  • Urgency to fundraise vs. long-term privacy
  • Trust via detail vs. oversharing of PHI
  • Public sympathy vs. scraping/secondary use

Risk path: disclosure → increased accessibility → secondary use/appropriation
Contextual Integrity: health context leaking into broad, remixable audiences

Prompts

Rights: Children have a say—ask first. Donors don’t need full medical details.

Risk: PHI spreads quickly. Reduce identifiers or share to Close Friends?

Norms: Many parents blur names & documents and limit audience when fundraising.

Assent

Helpers

  • Remove identifiers (blur names, MRNs, lab IDs; avoid clinic signage).
  • Non-identifying thumbnail
  • Save to draft and ask when the child is present.
  • Replace specifics with ranges (e.g., “weekly therapy,” not dates/codes).
  • Remix Off • Downloads Off • Location Off
  • Close Friends

Bystanders & co-ownership: blur other patients/parents; avoid nurse/clinic boards in frame.

Law & Policy

“Children have a right to be heard.” — UNCRC GC No. 12 “High privacy by default.” — ICO Children’s Code Assent in pediatrics — AAP (2016) / AAP (1995) HIPAA scope — applies to covered entities (providers/health plans), not parents; clinic footage can still expose PHI: HHS HIPAA Privacy Rule COPPA scope — regulates operators of online services collecting from <13s, not parents posting: FTC COPPA

Crowdfunding is usually outside HIPAA, but treat health details as sensitive: remove identifiers, avoid clinic signage, and limit audience/comments.

Evidence hooks

  • Solove, 2006: PHI posts often follow “disclosure → increased accessibility → secondary use” (profiling, scams, targeted ads).
  • Amon et al., 2022: Parents overshare to seek support; explicit permission from children is rare.
  • Steinberg, 2016: Children’s privacy/identity interests deserve independent weight even when parents speak for the family.
  • CNIL (France DPA): Official guidance warns about long-lived searchability and future discrimination from medical disclosures.
  • Stephenson et al., 2024: Virality/remix accelerate spread of health narratives beyond intended audiences.
